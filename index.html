Primary Source

  • Lightcast — aggregates job postings from multiple online platforms into a unified database
  • Complementary data via web scraping from major local portals:
    • Kenya & Nigeria: MyJobMag
    • Uganda: Jobline, Great Uganda Jobs

Data Construction

  • Dataset initially covers all occupations; NLP methods are used to identify AFS jobs and to extract and classify information from unstructured text, including skills and sector classifications (see Methods)
  • Postings deduplicated using semantic similarity of job descriptions
  • Required because Lightcast does not provide unique URLs for cross-source matching

Coverage of Online Vacancy Data and Digital Connectivity

  • Online job postings are a proxy for aggregate labour demand, especially in African labour markets, where they primarily capture recruitment in the formal, digitalized segment of the economy, while a substantial share of hiring still takes place through offline and informal channels (Chinen & Montorsi, 2025; World Bank, 2019).
  • The extent to which labour demand is observed online depends partly on digital connectivity and platform penetration, not only on underlying hiring activity
  • As a contextual measure, the ICT Development Index (IDI) is a 0–100 composite indicator of digital access, use, and skills
  • Online vacancy are likely to capture a larger share of total labour demand in more digitally connected markets, particularly in Kenya than in Nigeria or Uganda

AFS Job Classification

Keyword-based identification of agri-food system postings

Each job posting is classified as AFS based on keyword matching applied to job titles. Keywords are defined as regular expressions that capture morphological variants, compound terms, and domain-specific phrases (e.g. agricultur* matches agriculture, agricultural, agriculturist). A posting is flagged as AFS if any keyword from the on-farm or off-farm dictionaries matches.

Skill Extraction & Standardization Pipeline

Two-stage approach

1 Skill Extraction (LLM-based)
  • Language model applied to unstructured job descriptions
  • Extracts explicit and implicit skill candidates at sentence level
  • Designed to maximize recall
2 Skill Standardization (ESCO mapping)
  • Candidates mapped to 13,890 ESCO skills & knowledge concepts
  • Enables cross-country and cross-sector comparability
  • Enables aggregation of skills at different hierarchical levels
Context-aware matching
  • Combines skill candidate signal with full job description context
  • Uses sentence embeddings + cosine similarity
  • Retains top matches above a calibrated threshold
Model refinement
  • Fine-tuned on large synthetic dataset of annotated job ads
  • Uses contrastive learning
  • Improves disambiguation and robustness to noisy / implicit skills

Our pipeline outperforms the current state of the art (Decorte et al., 2025) by approx. 3 percentage points in RP@5 on the HOUSE and TECH benchmark datasets. These benchmarks consist of job postings annotated with ESCO gold-standard skill labels by human experts, against which model predictions are evaluated. Our approach achieves RP@5 scores of 0.822 and 0.838, respectively.

Skills Profile: On-Farm vs Off-Farm

ESCO Level 1 skill categories

On-farm jobs demand significantly more IT skills (50% vs 26%) and information skills, while off-farm roles emphasise assisting and caring (77% vs 59%) and handling and moving. The relatively high IT intensity in on-farm postings likely reflects selection effects in online vacancy data: digitally advertised on-farm positions tend to be more formalized and skill-intensive roles — such as irrigation engineering, precision agriculture, and farm management — rather than traditional low-skill agricultural work.
