Skills Profile: On-Farm vs Off-Farm

ESCO Level 1 skill categories · share of jobs containing each · z-test

On-farm jobs demand more IT skills (50% vs 26%) and information skills. Off-farm emphasises assisting/caring (77% vs 59%) and handling/moving.

Logistic Regression

P(on-farm) ~ skill categories + country FE · N = 33,000

Logistic Regression: Interpretation

Predicts On-Farm

  • S5 IT skills (OR = 2.78***) — precision agriculture, digital management
  • S7 Constructing (OR = 1.77***) — farm infrastructure
  • S2 Information (OR = 1.67***) — research, monitoring
  • T6 Life skills (OR = 1.68***) — entrepreneurship
  • S4 Management (OR = 1.42***) — farm operations

Predicts Off-Farm

  • S3 Caring (OR = 0.30***) — food service, hospitality
  • S6 Handling (OR = 0.64***) — supply chain, logistics
  • T1 Core skills (OR = 0.65***)
  • T3 Self-management (OR = 0.76***)
  • S8 Machinery (OR = 0.89***)
Pseudo R² = 0.165. Country effects are large: Nigeria is strongly off-farm oriented (OR = 0.39), Uganda is on-farm (OR = 2.81).

Key Takeaways

  1. AFS is growing — the share of agri-food jobs in online postings increased across all three countries (2021–2023).
  2. On-farm ≠ off-farm skills — distinctly different skill profiles. On-farm demands digital, analytical, and management skills; off-farm centres on manual handling, food preparation, and caring.
  3. Digital agriculture is real — IT skills (S5) are the strongest predictor of on-farm jobs (OR = 2.78), suggesting rapid digitalization of primary agriculture.
  4. Country heterogeneity matters — Nigeria's AFS is more off-farm/processing oriented; Uganda and Kenya lean on-farm. Policy responses should be tailored.
  5. TVET implications — training programmes for on-farm youth should integrate IT, data analysis, and farm management; off-farm programmes should focus on food safety, processing technology, and supply chain operations.
